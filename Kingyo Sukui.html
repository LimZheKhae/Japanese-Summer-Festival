Kingyo Sukui

A traditional Japanese game in which a person scoops goldfish using a paper scooper is known as goldfish scooping (金魚すくい, 金魚掬い, Kingyo-sukui). It's also known as "Snatching Goldfish," "Dipping for Goldfish," and "Scooping Goldfish." "Kingyo" refers to goldfish, whereas "sukui" refers to scooping. Instead of goldfish, bouncy balls are sometimes utilised. Summer festivals in Japan, known as ennichi, frequently contain a stall dedicated to this practise.


Rules

Each player has his or her own game. The main rule is that the player uses a paper scooper called a "poi" to scoop goldfish from a pool and place them in a bowl with the poi. Because the poi can quickly tear, this game necessitates caution and speed. When the poi is fully shattered or incapable of scooping properly, the game is done. Even if one section of the poi is torn, the game can still be played with the remaining poi.

The game is not a competition at ennichi or summer festival stalls. The scooped goldfish can be taken home in a plastic bag given by the stall keeper, and participation usually costs around 100 yen. Players can scoop till their pois are entirely broken because the game is limitless. If they are unable to scoop any goldfish, the stall owner may provide them one or two free goldfish. Typically, each stall has its own set of rules. For example, there are various stalls where players can spend more to receive a stronger poi. Other stalls reward gamers with gifts if they scoop a large amount of money. Medaka (Japanese killifish) are a type of goldfish that is faster and more difficult to catch than goldfish. One medaka is usually found for every four goldfish, so catching one counts as four goldfish in ennichi.

Players compete in the National Goldfish Scooping Championship using official regulations that differ from those stated above.

Pool

The goldfish are usually kept in a small plastic pool with a surface area of 1 square metre (11 sq ft) and a depth of 20 centimetres (7.9 in).

Poi (ポイ, poi)

A spherical plastic frame with a hand grip and paper on the frame make up the poi. Because the poi paper can easily shatter when submerged in water, players should not move the poi too quickly. Poi paper is divided into several categories. No. 7 (7号), on the other hand, is weaker, while No. 5 (5号), on the other hand, is stronger. Staff have unbreakable poi, which consist of a net to scoop goldfish instead of paper, in some stalls.

Bowl

The bowl used to store the scooped goldfish is normally made of plastic and is in the shape of a semi-sphere with a capacity of about.5 litres (0.88 imp pt; 1.1 US pt).

Bag

Players can take goldfish home in bags provided by the stall keeper if they win.

Goldfish

The varieties of goldfish often used in goldfish scooping are "Koaka", "Demekin", and "Anekin".


History

This game started in the late Edo period, around 1810. Pois were manufactured with nets back then, and it was a popular children's pastime. Pois were first created from paper in the Taishō period, approximately 1910, and stalls were established.

The game became more and more popular, and the National Goldfish Scooping Championship began in 1995. Today it is so popular that stalls can be found at many ennichi 縁日 "summer festivals" in Japan.
