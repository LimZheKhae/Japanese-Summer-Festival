Festival Foods

Enjoy eating mouth-watering delicacies.



Karaage

Karaage (唐揚げ, 空揚げ, or から揚げ, [kaɾa aɡe]) is a Japanese cooking technique in which various dishes are deep fried in oil, most commonly chicken, but sometimes other meats and seafood. The method is delicately coating small pieces of meat or fish in flour, potato or corn starch, then frying them in a light oil. Prior to coating, the meals are marinated. The procedure differs from that of tempura, which is not marinated and coated with a batter. Karaage is frequently eaten alone or with shredded cabbage and rice.


Origin

The Toyoken restaurant in Beppu, Japan, is credited with popularising Karaage in the 1920s. The method gained popularity as a result of post-World War II food shortages in Japan, particularly for chicken. Chicken was already a popular dinner, but the Karaage process made it easier to prepare and gave it a new flavour profile.

The manner of cooking has its origins in Chinese cuisine, and it is remarkably similar to how the Chinese fried tofu for vegetarian foods. The frying method, on the other hand, has been around since the Edo period of Japan's tempura. In a similar way to karaage, the Chinese utilise soy sauce and rice wine.

The dish has become popular throughout Japan since the 1920s. Because fried chicken has become the most popular variant of the cooking style, karaage is usually used to refer to it. As a quick food item, karaage (chicken version) is widely accessible in convenience stores like as Lawson, FamilyMart, and 7-Eleven. It's also widely accessible in food stalls across the country.

Tatsuta-age is the name given to it when it is covered in starch. Tatsuta is a river, although there are several explanations as to why it was given that name.

Karaage can also be found at festivals and food stalls all around Japan. The annual Karaage Festival in Oita is one such event, with over 60 different establishments participating to offer distinct renditions of the Japanese delicacy.


Regional karaage

Since karaage's popularity extended across Japan, various regional variations have emerged, the most noteworthy of which are:

  • Zangi – Hokkaido prefecture's version of karaage, made with a marinade and served with a spicy dipping sauce.
  • Tebasaki – Nagoya’s version of karaage, made with bone in chicken wings, sprinkled with sesame seeds and basted with a special sauce.
  • Toriten – Oita prefecture’s version of karaage, coated in wheat flour and often used as a topping for udon noodles.
  • Chicken nanban – Miyazaki prefecture’s version of karaage, dipped in sweet vinegar and topped with tartar sauce.
  • Gurukun no kara-age – Okinawa prefecture’s version of karaage. Gurukun is Okinawa’s official and most popular fish, often called a “banana fish”, it is a fish fried whole and served with lemon.
  • Dakgangjeong – Korea's take on fried chicken, very similar to karaage, but usually made with milk and a sort of sweet/spicy sauce consisting of soy sauce, rice wine, red chili pepper paste, honey, and seasonings.
