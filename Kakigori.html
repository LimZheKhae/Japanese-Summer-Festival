Kakigori

Kakigri (かき氷) is a shaved ice delicacy from Japan that is flavoured with syrup and a sweetener, usually condensed milk.


History

The origins of kakigōri can be traced back to the Heian period in Japanese history, when Japanese nobles would shave blocks of ice saved during the colder months and serve it with sweet syrup in the summer. The Pillow Book, a book of observations written by Sei Shōnagon, who served the Imperial Court during the Heian dynasty, mentions Kakigōri's origin. In the nineteenth century, as ice became more freely available to the general population throughout the summer, Kakigōri became more accessible. In Yokohama, the first Kakigōri store is thought to have opened in 1869.

In Japan, July 25 is known as kakigōri day because its pronunciation sounds similar to summer ice. Another explanation for July 25 being kakigōri day is that it was a record high temperature in Japan on that day in 1933.


Description

A hand-cranked machine spins a block of ice over a shaving blade in the traditional method of producing kakigōri. Despite the prevalence of electric shavers, street merchants can still be seen hand-shaving ice blocks in the summer.

Kakigōri is usually made of pure ice, which is often a frozen block of mineral water, rather than flavoured ice. The ice is usually obtained from natural springs and then tempered to produce the best shaving quality. Prior to refrigeration, kakigōri  ice was traditionally stored in mountain caves or ice homes.

It's similar to a snow cone, but with a few key distinctions: It has a smoother, fluffier ice quality, similar to freshly fallen snow, and is virtually always eaten with a spoon. Kakigri is distinguished from other shaved ice desserts by the texture of the ice. Because of the contrast in texture, it has also been translated as 'Angel Snow,' which sounds more enticing.

Strawberry, cherry, lemon, green tea, grape, melon, "Blue Hawaii," sweet plum, and colourless syrup are all popular flavours. Some stores use two or more syrups to create colourful variations. Condensed or evaporated milk is frequently sprinkled on top of kakigri to sweeten it.

Kakigōri is served in festivals, convenience stores, coffee shops, specialty kakigōri parlours, and restaurants in addition to street stalls. It's also widely produced at home by families. During the hot summer months, kakigōri is widely available throughout Japan. Often served alongside other street delicacies such Japanese yakisoba, takoyaki, and cotton candy at summer festivals and fairs such as matsuri and bon odori festivals. In Japan, kakigōri is one of the summer attractions. It's served with ice cream and sweetened red beans or tapioca pearls in some places.

A flag with the kanji sign for ice kōri (氷) is a frequent and traditional technique for a business to advertise that it serves kakigōri.
