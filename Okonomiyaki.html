Okonomiyaki

Okonomiyaki (Japanese: お好み焼き) is a savoury Japanese pancake meal made with wheat flour batter and other (combined or as toppings) ingredients baked on a teppan (flat griddle). Cabbage, beef, and shellfish are common ingredients, with okonomiyaki sauce (made with Worcestershire sauce), aonori (dried seaweed flakes), katsuobushi (bonito flakes), Japanese mayonnaise, and pickled ginger as toppings.

Okonomiyaki is most commonly identified with two unique versions from Hiroshima or the Kansai region of Japan, but it is widely accessible across the country, with toppings and batters differing by region. The name comes from the Japanese words okonomi, which means "how you like" or "what you prefer," and yaki, which means "cooked." Konamono (Konamon in Kansai dialect), or flour-based Japanese food, is an example.

Monjayaki is a liquid-based okonomiyaki popular in Kyoto. Outside of Japan, street sellers can be seen serving it in Manila, Taipei, Bangkok, and Jakarta.


History

Funoyaki [ja], a thin crêpe-like delicacy, may have been an early forerunner to okonomiyaki. Despite the dish's contents are unknown, they may have included fu. Records of the word funoyaki date back to the 16th century, as written about by tea expert Sen no Rikyū, and though the dish's ingredients are uncertain, it may have included fu (wheat gluten).  Funoyaki was a thin crêpe baked on a cooking pot with miso basted on one side by the late Edo era (1603–1867). This pastry is the forerunner of the current confections kintsuba (金つば), also known as gintsuba (銀つば) in Kyoto and Osaka, and taiko-yaki (also known as imagawayaki), both of which contain nerian (練り餡), a sweet bean paste.

Monjiyaki (文字焼き), a related confection, was popular with children at dagashiya (駄菓子屋), stores selling cheap sweets during the Meiji era (1868–1912). This was prepared by using flour batter to make letters (monji) or pictures on a teppan (iron griddle) and then adding things of choice. The confectionery was also known as dondonyaki (どんどん焼き), which was derived from the onomatopoeia of stall vendors playing drums to attract customers.

In the 1930s, the word "okonomiyaki" had its first appearance in an Osaka shop. Cooking these crêpes became a pastime after the 1923 Great Kantō earthquake, when people lacked utilities, and following World War II (when rice was scarce), okonomiyaki arose as an economical and substantial dish for people of all ages, frequently with savoury toppings such as pork, seafood, and vegetables.  During the "okonomiyaki boom," commercially available domestic equipment and ingredients for the dish became available. Monjiyaki has also evolved into the comparable modern cuisine monjayaki (モンジャ焼き), which has a runnier batter due to the addition of additional water and a different cooked consistency.

In the Taishō period (1912–1926), Kyoto's issen yōshoku [ja] (cheap Western-style cuisine) may have produced an early variant of current savoury okonomiyaki in the form of a pancake with Worcestershire sauce and chopped scallion.
